# My World: Designing an AI That Remembers You Back

**Role:** Product Designer (design, prompt engineering, prototyping, co-implementation)
**Company:** COROS AI, a pre-seed ontological AI coaching startup ("Conversations that transform")
**Team:** Founder/CEO, 3 engineers, 2 designers. I owned My World's design end to end.
**Timeline:** [X months], 2026 (**feature currently in progress, shipping incrementally**)
**Tools:** Figma, Claude Code, Streamlit, HTML/CSS/JS prototyping, LLM prompt engineering

---

## The problem: coaching that evaporates

COROS is an AI life coach. Users have genuinely transformative conversations: they name a fear, make a commitment, see something about themselves for the first time. Then the session ends and all of it disappears into chat history.

My World is the answer: a page that reflects a user's coaching history back to them. The design brief I wrote for it became the team's north star: **a mirror, not a filing cabinet.** Not a productivity tracker, not a stats dashboard: a surface that shows you the pattern of your own transformation and invites you back into conversation.

That one sentence ended up settling dozens of downstream debates: no page-level filters, no scores or streaks, a ~100-word cap per widget, and every element ending in an on-ramp back to coaching (Continue, Revisit, New conversation).

---

## What made this hard

This wasn't a UI project with an API behind it. The content of every card is **generated by LLM prompts I wrote**, so the design and the intelligence had to be built together. A beautiful breakthrough card is worthless if the model hallucinates breakthroughs. I ended up owning three layers at once:

1. **The information architecture**: how raw conversations become structured, browsable objects
2. **The interface**: a responsive web + mobile page of independent widgets
3. **The prompts**: the generation layer that fills every field on screen, plus the QA tooling to test it

---

## Layer 1: The architecture (Sessions → Topics → My World)

I co-authored the product's design and implementation document through four versions (v1 → v4), which the engineering team builds directly from. Its foundational distinction: a **Session** is an event (one conversation, factually summarized), a **Topic** is a territory (a situation in your life that sessions keep returning to: "Navigating a difficult conversation with dad," "The job search").

The hardest rule I fought for: **a topic is a situational territory, never a feeling.** Early model outputs kept producing topics like "Self-worth" and "Avoiding conflict": psychologically flattering, navigationally useless, and subtly harmful (nobody wants their anxiety enshrined as a permanent folder). I codified this as the *identity-vs-texture* rule: feelings may color a topic's summary, but the title and organizing identity is always the situation. This rule now lives in the topic prompts and the detection pipeline (embedding search at a tuned threshold → LLM match decision).

I also designed My World as a **federated portal**: each widget is engineered and launched independently with its own prompt, logic, and documented rationale. At a pre-seed startup this was a shipping strategy as much as a design one: engineers could build the Reminders widget without waiting on the Provocation prompt to stabilize.

## Layer 2: The interface (three iterations that mattered)

### Establishing the top level

The first structural decision was the page itself: a **featured band** of three reflective cards (My Breakthroughs, Coaching Provocation, Latest Breakthrough) above a full-width stacked **Topics list**, filterable by the seven life dimensions (Family, Work, Self, Health, Meaning, Belonging, World) and by status (Active / Snoozed / Archived). Topics are never tiled as a grid; each gets room to breathe, because a topic is a respected territory, not a search-history tile.

A cross-page filtering concept (click a dimension, everything filters) tested well in principle, but I argued to defer it out of v1 and hold a stricter rule: **filters belong to individual widgets, not the page.** That constraint paid off later in an unexpected way.

### Merging the donut and the Latest Breakthrough

We had two separate widgets: a donut chart counting breakthroughs by dimension, and a card showing the most recent breakthrough. The donut was under threat: half the team leaned toward replacing it with a line chart, and a mockup decision was pending on me.

Instead of choosing between them, I merged them into **one widget with two modes**, and specced the full state machine:

- **Mode A (default):** donut inert, panel shows the globally newest breakthrough, arrows walk the entire history chronologically.
- **Mode B (segment clicked):** panel filters to that dimension; arrows traverse a flat list built from all non-empty dimensions in color-wheel palette order, newest→oldest within each; **auto-advance across a dimension boundary moves the donut's live selection in real time.** Click outside to exit; reload always restores Mode A.

I resolved every edge case before handoff: backward symmetry (stepping before a dimension's newest lands on the previous dimension's oldest), wrap behavior at the ends, empty dimensions absent from both the ring and the traversal, and the zero-breakthrough empty state. The merge also quietly resolved two open debates at once: it settled donut-vs-line-chart in the donut's favor (a clickable segmented ring earns its place as a filter), and because the filter is now *intra-widget*, it delivered the cross-filtering experience without violating the no-page-level-filters rule.

### Decluttering the topic surface

Through weekly design triage with the founder and stakeholders, I ran repeated simplification passes on the topic card and detail view: stripping section labels down to inline values (`Family · May 9 – May 30 · Active`), removing timestamps, showing the year only when it isn't the current one, renaming "Archive" to "Box" to fit the product's ontological language (with the icon arrow flipped to point *into* the box), and holding the line on sticky bottom CTAs, because users make their "continue or close" decision only *after* reading through a topic. Small calls individually; together they turned a dense admin panel into something that reads like a document.

### The Reminders widget

When the team reframed `commitments` (concrete checkable tasks) into `reminders` (which can also be stances and ways of being: "Notice the voice of fear and keep going anyway"), I designed the widget that carries them: reminders pooled across all sessions, newest first, **paginated inside the widget** (never a separate page). Two actions: **Done** and **Delete**. Delete exists precisely because these are AI-generated and the AI can be wrong, so users need an honest way out. Both actions update shared state across the widget *and* the originating session view.

One detail I'm proud of: when a user un-checks a completed reminder, it **re-enters at its original chronological position**, not floated to the top. Position encodes creation order and is invariant to toggling: one rule, consistent across both surfaces. I flagged the engineering implication myself: since done items vanish from the widget, correct reinsertion requires storing the original creation timestamp in shared state, not deriving it from visible position. That note went straight into the build spec.

The design is fully **responsive**: every widget, the topic list, and both detail views adapt from a 1440px desktop layout down to mobile, specced with breakpoint behavior rather than as two separate designs.

## Layer 3: The AI (prompts, playgrounds, and QA)

### Prompt engineering as design work

I wrote and iterated the prompts that generate everything on screen: the session summary (through six versions), the topic summary (through seven), topic detection, and the coaching invitation prompt. The craft here is closer to spec-writing than copywriting: every sentence must contain an executable instruction, and I learned the hard way that **silence in a gate is an admission**: if you don't explicitly name an exclusion with examples, the model will walk through it.

The `breakthrough_insight` field is the best example. A breakthrough shown back to a user is a claim about their life. Get it wrong and you manufacture a false memory. I built the field around a strict **ownership gate** (it only qualifies if the user explicitly said it or strongly agreed), a Promise/Declaration test, an explicit ban list for inceptive language ("began to," "starting to": a breakthrough is an arrival, not an intention), a ban on victory language ("freedom from," "dissolves its grip"), and a required verb-first opener to kill stative constructions.

### Building my own QA tooling

You can't QA a generative feature by eyeballing one output. I extended our **Streamlit playground** (a test harness that runs real conversation transcripts through the full pipeline and exports structured JSON) and specced a new Invitation tab (one new file, a five-line additive diff to the existing UI, shared helpers imported rather than duplicated) so I could tune the provocation prompt against real session objects with the context window size as the primary dial.

My QA method: run a batch of ~20 sessions, then perform **field-level compliance checks** of every output against the prompt's own explicit rules. A recent run surfaced five must-fix issues (including the breakthrough gate passing two "non-seeings," a near-verbatim use of banned psychological register, and a markdown artifact leaking into user-facing prose), each traced to a specific missing clause and fixed with a surgical prompt insert, not a rewrite. ## Co-implementation: from mockups to merge requests

Midway through the project I stopped ending my work at the Figma handoff. Using **Claude Code**, I began implementing widgets myself on localhost, [stack detail: e.g., React + Tailwind on our shadcn-based Obra design system], and shipping working builds directly to engineers for review [detail: PRs / branch handoff to Maaz and Zain].

The technique that made this work: I write **implementation prompts the way I write design specs.** The combined breakthrough widget went to code as a complete state-machine prompt: both modes, all transitions, the traversal model, the edge cases, design tokens, and every assumption explicitly flagged as a one-line toggle if wrong. Ambiguity resolved before code, not during review.

The payoff was speed: design questions that used to take a build-and-wait cycle now get answered the same afternoon, and engineers review a working reference instead of interpreting a static frame. [Metric if usable: "reduced design-to-review cycles from days to hours."]

I applied the same rigor to the design system itself: a seven-hue dimension palette (rose → fuchsia, one hue per color-wheel zone) with three semantic tokens per dimension referencing the brand layer, which is the same token order that drives the breakthrough widget's traversal logic, so the palette is a single source of truth for both visual identity and interaction behavior.

## What I'd want a hiring team to take from this

I designed a novel AI product surface from architecture to pixels, but the thing I'd point to is the shape of the work: I wrote the product doc engineers build from, the prompts that generate the content, the QA tooling that validates them, the specs that resolve edge cases before code, and increasingly the code itself. At a pre-seed startup, the most valuable designer is the one who removes handoffs. That's the designer I've become, and the design engineer I'm becoming.
